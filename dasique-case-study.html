The Problem

Same product page. Two different shoppers.

Dasique's product descriptions were direct-translated from the domestic market. The original copy led with texture metaphors and mood—“a mermaid's magic, pressed into every shade.” North American shoppers open a product page and scan for something else entirely: shade count, wear time, whether it transfers.

The pages weren't badly written. They were answering questions the majority of the audience wasn't asking.

Design Thinking — PMPO Framework

How I think about product design decisions.

Every design decision in this project maps to a product metric. Here's how I framed each one.

Aha Moment
What makes a shopper stay?
The original pages buried functional info under poetic copy. NA shoppers' Aha Moment is seeing specs they can evaluate—shade count, wear time, transfer claim. I moved that to the first scroll.
“How did you define the success metric for your onboarding UX?”
Activation Funnel
Reducing Time-to-Aha
Browse → Read Copy → Add to Cart. The friction was in step 2: copy that didn't answer purchase questions. Rewriting removed the gap between landing and converting.
“What design decision did you make to reduce Time-to-Aha?”
Retention Detail
Microcopy as trust signal
Edge cases matter: “transfer-proof,” “no creasing,” “stays through coffee.” These micro-details are what separate a bounce from a bookmark. Each line was tested against real shopper objections.
“Show me the level of detail in your design work.”
Living Artifact
Hypothesis-driven iteration
I didn't receive a spec and execute. I observed shoppers, built a hypothesis about the copy gap, benchmarked competitors, and iterated on the framework before writing a single product page.
“Are you a designer who takes PRDs, or one who defines the problem?”
4 Product Risks
Valuable · Usable · Feasible · Viable
Valuable: 64% NA traffic with KR-optimized copy. Usable: Copy scannability for mobile shoppers. Feasible: 18 pages in 1.5 weeks, solo. Viable: Zero dev cost—content-only change.
“Why this direction? Walk me through the trade-offs.”
Business Impact
Design decisions with measurable outcomes
Copy redesign targets the conversion gap between browse and cart. Staff training reduces onboarding friction from days to minutes. Both deliverables directly address churn at different funnel stages.
“How would you expect this design change to affect churn?”
The Process

6.5 weeks, four phases.

Weeks 1–4
User Research
Behavioral observation
Observed international customers at the flagship location. Coded behavior patterns—what they asked, where they hesitated, what made them leave.
Week 5
Content Redesign
Competitive benchmarking
Rewrote 18 product pages. Benchmarked against Rhode, Dior, and Laneige to identify how NA-facing brands structure product information.
Week 6
Systems Design
Operational UX
Built a mobile staff training guide—8 customer scenarios with English scripts and pronunciation. Replaced a fragmented workflow with one shared URL.
Week 6.5
Market Research
Strategic pivot
NA expansion research. Started with industry reports, pivoted mid-week to a consumer-perspective framework after finding the data too supply-side.
What I Learned

Localization isn't translation. It's redesigning for a different mental model.

The product was the same. The quality was the same. But each audience had a completely different framework for evaluating it. One group responds to mood and sensory language. The other needs specs, wear time, and performance claims they can verify.

Designing for global users means understanding that the same screen needs to answer fundamentally different questions depending on who's looking at it.
